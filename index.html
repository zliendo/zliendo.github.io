Improving
Emergency Triage

IDANN Triage
utilizes interpretable deep attention neural networks
to predict critical patient outcomes and resources,
aiding emergency departments with patient prioritization.

Challenge

Triage is the assessment of the degree of urgency or acuity of a patient’s need for treatment. This process occurs whenever a patient enters in the emergency department. Prioritizing patients is a challenge for emergency departments all over the world, as departments need to balance ever-changing patient needs, wait times, and hospital resources. One significant issue is the inability to quickly distinguish significantly ill patients; under-triaging these patients can delay time-sensitive treatment. With improved assessments of ER patient risk and needs, emergency department (ED) nurses can realize improved triage assessments, reducing the incidence of under-triaging.

The ESI and the Central Problem

The ESI is a prioritization tool used by the majority of hospitals in the US, assessing patient needs based on the severity of the patient’s condition and the anticipated number of hospital resources needed to complete treatment. Under the ESI guidelines, patients receive a priority rating from 1 to 5, with 1 being the most urgent.

While the ESI usefully codifies the triage decision-making process, over-triaging (assigning a lower ESI rating than optimal) and under-triaging remain problems. Over-triaging means that patients who could wait are given higher priority than necessary, possibly to the exclusion of more urgent cases. Under-triaging is potentially more serious, as it implies that the condition of the individual needs more urgent attention than they are slated to receive.

A natural outgrowth of this phenomenon is that the majority of patients are triaged to the middle category, ESI 3. Overutilization of ESI 3 necessarily means that the other ESI categories are underutilized, which is especially problematic for patients who need a more urgent category.

While the majority of patients did not experience a critical outcome (CO), we can see that the majority of CO patients were assigned to ESI 3. The majority of ICU (Intensive Care Unit), OR (Operation Room), and cardiac catheterization patients were likewise assigned to the overutilized ESI 3. We found that 39.9% of patients who experienced a critical outcome were correctly assigned to ESI 1 or ESI 2. Of those who died in the ED, 36% were assigned to ESI 3 or lower.

Product

Decrease fatalities and improve outcomes by assisting triage nurses to avoid errors in categorization.

Approach

We are using deep learning models trained on historical emergency department records to jointly predict which patients are at high risk for critical outcomes, and how many hospital resources each patient is expected to require. Based on these predictions, we are able to recommend an ESI prioritization rating, and show the most important factors for this recommendation. This rating relies on data that ER nurses already collect during triage, so this rating allows nurses to effectively leverage the collective wisdom of thousands of emergency department cases, without disrupting the flow from triage assessment to ESI assignment.

AI Model

As mentioned earlier, the biggest problem with the ESI is that it relies on the triage practitioner’s ability to make accurate assessments as to the criticality of patients and the number of resources required for their care. IDANN helps avoid miscategorization by providing an objective prediction of both of these parameters, thus allowing practitioners to leverage case history in their decision making. As its name suggests, IDANN utilizes neural networks for its predictions and pairs them with text embedding and an attention layer for interpretability.

Model Rationale Explanation

While neural networks can have significant performance advantages compared to other models, especially with large datasets, they suffer from being hard to understand by end-users. IDANN addresses this by including an attention layer that ranks the importance of the predictors and the reason for visit (RFV) embeddings. These rankings are then handed back to the requesting application for interpretation

GUI vizualizations

One of the most important aspects of IDANN lies in its ability to explain its decision-making to the end-users. In medicine, more so than other fields, it is important to ensure that the end-users are able to leverage the advantages provided by this tool without having to make a leap of faith in trusting in it blindly. IDANN uses visualizations to provide the end-user with a concise, easy-to-follow explanation of the decision-making process. These visualizations will help providers gain confidence in IDANN’s predictions and to take be able to weigh the relative impact of different predictors on the patient

API service

While an application like IDANN would be valuable on its own, one of its most prominent features is the ability to embed it into other applications through the use of a REST API. This standard-based approach ensures that EMR integrations are straightforward, and additional functionality and integrations can be pursued freely. The use of FHIR helps address data portability and integrity concerns.



Model

Data Sources

Our modeling data is drawn from the National Hospital Ambulatory Medical Care Survey (NHAMCS), collected by the Centers for Disease Control (CDC). The data represented therein are comprised of de-identified health records from 1992-2015, drawn from a national sample of visits to emergency departments, about 24K records a year. An independent investigation of the dataset by Dugas et al. (2016) found the data to be complete and representative.

Due to the availability of certain key variables, and to offset issues of population drift in certain health measurements, we utilized the datasets from 2009-2015 in our modeling. Based on Dugas et al.’s findings, we also excluded non-adults (younger than 18 years) and cases with unknown age, patients dead on arrival, cases transferred to a psychiatric unit, and cases for whom the final outcome or prioritization rating were unknown. We believe that the final study cohort of 24,321 patient visits peryear provides data for a nationally representative sample of 141.4 million visits per year.

Feature Engineering



We categorized vital signs by known thresholds of danger that indicate things like low oxygen, hypothermic temperature, or dangerous heart rate. Normalizing the vital signs scales was critical, since things like temperature and heart rate have enormously different potential ranges. We also grouped the reasons for ED visit (RFV) into categories that indicate some complaints that are known to be dangerous or high-priority, like fever, shortness of breath, or chest pain. These thresholds and categories were based on the descriptions provided in Dugas et al., “An Electronic Emergency Triage System to Improve Patient Distribution by Critical Outcomes”, 2016. We also flagged diabetes as a condition, as well as the metropolitan location of the ED that each patient visited.


Reasons for Visit (RFV) Codes as vectors and embeddings

A key feature of the RFV codes was that they have a hierarchical coded arrangement; we transformed them into vectors in order to capture that hierarchy. We additionally translated the RFV codes into their text form, and created text embeddings in order to capture the semantic similarities between complaints that would be obvious to a nurse.

 
Outcome variables

To calculate the Critical Outcome variable, we collapsed several patient outcomes into one binary variable: any patient an outcome of death, admittance to ICU, OR, or cardiac catheterization, was considered to have experienced a critical outcome. This represented about 7.1% of the total dataset, which we accounted for with weighted modeling techniques. To calculate Resources, in alignment with the definition outline by the ESI, we calculated the total resources required for each patient, among diagnostic methods and procedures available in the dataset. Ultimately, this resulted in patients requiring 0, 1, or ≥2 resources.

Machine Learning Methods

We used a 10 fold cross validation and area under receiver-operator characteristic curve (AUC ROC) to measure the performance of all our models. We found that AUC ROC was the metric used by published papers performing predictive modeling with electronic health records (EHR). Because the data is imbalance, with most of the samples not resulting on a critical outcome, we used the class_weight option in all our models which implicitly balanced the data.

  • Baseline Model: Logistic Regression (LR)

    Our baseline was the replication of the Logistic Regression model implemented by Dugas et al. (2016) , which uses the same data source (CDC) we are using in our model. We replicated features and matched the AUC ROC reported. This starting baseline allowed us to confirm we are using the correct fields from the CDC files.

  • Adding New Features

    The next step was adding features that we thought would improve the model performance, features like the RFV codes as vectors and some patient medical history available during triage. The LR model performance improved.

  • Random Forest (RF)

    Another traditional machine learning model that we tried was a Random forest (RF) which provides a list of feature importance, relevant for model interpretation. The RF model performance was lower than the LR model though.

  • How about Deep Learning?

    Considering that medical data is not linearly separable and Logistic Regression cannot handle well this type of data, we implemented Deep Neural Networks. It is well known that Deep Neural Networks perform better with enough data for training and we thought that we had enough data to try.

  • Forward Neural Network (FNN)

    We started with a Forward Neural Network, feeding the same features used by the Logistic Regression model and indeed its performance was higher.

  • FNN with text Embeddings

    Our next model was a FNN that included the training of embedding for the Reason for Visit (RFV) codes, performing better than all previous models.

Prediction

The predictor leverages the trained models to generate an ESI rating and associated supporting data.

IDANN follows the official definition of ESI as closely as possible to ensure that its use conforms with established emergency department practices, thus minimizing the implementation risk and allowing for faster deployment with minimal disruption.

Apis

REST API

A client application passes a JSON package with all relevant information to the API, which then executes an instance of the predictor function. The result of the predictor function is then passed back to the API service and ultimately the client application, which will leverage the ESI as a suggestion and provide visualizations explaining the decision.
