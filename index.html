IDANN Triage

Improving Emergency Triage through
interpretable deep attention neural networks
to predict critical patient outcomes and resources.

Challenge

Triage is the assessment of the degree of urgency or acuity of a patient’s need for treatment. This process occurs whenever a patient enters in the emergency department. Prioritizing patients is a challenge for emergency departments all over the world. One significant issue is the inability to quickly distinguish significantly ill patients; under-triaging these patients can delay time-sensitive treatment.

The ESI and the Central Problem



Emergency Severity Index (ESI): a prioritization tool used by the majority of hospitals in the US, rating patients by severity of condition and resources needed

ESI
1 Life-saving measures needed immediately
2 High risk of a time-sensitive critical condition
3 Stable, with multiple resources needed for treatment
4 Stable, with only one resource needed for treatment
5 Stable, with no anticipated resource use
  • Over-triaging (assigning a more urgent ESI rating than needed) and under-triaging remain problems
  • Under-triaging is more serious, since it implies that an individual’s condition requires more urgent attention than they are queued to receive
  • So what happens? Overutilization of the middle ESI 3 category

We found that 39.9% of patients who experienced a critical outcome were correctly assigned to ESI 1 or ESI 2. Of those who died in the ED, 36% were assigned to ESI 3.

Product

Decrease fatalities and improve outcomes by assisting triage nurses to avoid errors in categorization.

Machine Learning Model

IDANN Triage utilizes neural networks for its predictions and pairs them with text embeddings and an attention layer for interpretability. The model relies on data that ER nurses already collect during triage, allowing nurses to leverage the collective wisdom of thousands of emergency department cases, without disrupting the flow from triage assessment to ESI assignment. This tool helps avoid triage miscategorization by using deep learning models to jointly predict the risk of critical outcome and the resources needed for each patient

Modeling Rationale

Neural networks often outperform other types of models, especially with large datasets, but can be hard for both data science experts and end-users to understand. In medicine, it is especially important that end-users are able to take advantage of machine learning tools in concert with their own expertise. IDANN addresses this by including an attention layer, which ranks the importance of the predictors and the embedded reasons for visit.

GUI Visualizations

IDANN uses intuitive visualizations to provide concise, easy-to-follow explanations of the model’s recommendation. With these visualizations, care providers can gain confidence in IDANN's predictions, and weigh the relative impact of different predictors for each patient. These visualizations are made possible by the inclusion of the attention layer.

API Service

IDANN Triage can be embedded in other applications via REST API. This standard-based and FHIR-compliant service ensures seamless integration with electronic medical record applications. IDANN visualizations can be also embedded on EHR web pages via a Javascript API.

Model

Data Sources

Our modeling data is drawn from the National Hospital Ambulatory Medical Care Survey (NHAMCS), collected by the Centers for Disease Control (CDC). The data represented therein are comprised of de-identified health records from 1992-2015, drawn from a national sample of visits to emergency departments, about 24K records a year. The data are part of routine ED data collection, so any ED could provide the same fields. An independent investigation of the dataset by Dugas et al. (2016) found the data to be complete and representative sample of 141.4 million visits per year. We based our exclusion categories on the categories outlined in Dugas et al.

Feature Engineering



Vital signs are categorized by known thresholds of danger, and normalized due to different scales of measurement. We also grouped the reasons for ED visit (RFV) into categories that indicate some complaints that are known to be dangerous or high-priority. These thresholds and categories were based on the descriptions provided in Dugas et al., “An Electronic Emergency Triage System to Improve Patient Distribution by Critical Outcomes”, 2016. In addition to the features suggested by Degas et al., we included flags for diabetes, chronic heart failure, the patient’s metropolitan area, and additional usage of the RFV codes.


Reasons for Visit (RFV) Codes as vectors and embeddings

A key feature of the RFV codes was that they have a hierarchical coded arrangement; we transformed them into vectors in order to capture that hierarchy. We additionally translated the RFV codes into their text form, and created text embeddings in order to capture the semantic similarities between complaints that would be obvious to a nurse.

 
Outcome variables

To calculate the Critical Outcome variable, we collapsed several patient outcomes into one binary variable: any patient an outcome of death, admittance to ICU, OR, or cardiac catheterization, was considered to have experienced a critical outcome. This represented about 7.1% of the total dataset, which we accounted for with weighted modeling techniques. To calculate Resources, in alignment with the definition outline by the ESI, we calculated the total resources required for each patient, among diagnostic methods and procedures available in the dataset. Ultimately, this resulted in patients requiring 0, 1, or ≥2 resources.

Machine Learning Methods

We used a 10 fold cross validation and area under receiver-operator characteristic curve (AUC ROC) to measure the performance of all our models. We found that AUC ROC was the metric used by published papers performing predictive modeling with electronic health records (EHR). Because the data is imbalance, with most of the samples not resulting on a critical outcome, we used the class_weight option in all our models which implicitly balanced the data.

  • Baseline Model: Logistic Regression (LR)

    Our baseline was the replication of the Logistic Regression model implemented by Dugas et al. (2016) , which uses the same data source (CDC) we are using in our model. We replicated features and matched the AUC ROC reported. This starting baseline allowed us to confirm we are using the correct fields from the CDC files.

  • Adding New Features

    The next step was adding features that we thought would improve the model performance, features like the RFV codes as vectors and some patient medical history available during triage. The LR model performance improved.

  • Random Forest (RF)

    Another traditional machine learning model that we tried was a Random forest (RF) which provides a list of feature importance, relevant for model interpretation. The RF model performance was lower than the LR model though.

  • How about Deep Learning?

    Considering that medical data is not linearly separable and Logistic Regression cannot handle well this type of data, we implemented Deep Neural Networks (DNN). It is well known that DNN perform better with enough data for training and we thought that we had enough data to try.

  • Forward Neural Network (FNN) with text Embeddings

    We started with a Forward Neural Network, feeding the same features used by the Logistic Regression model and indeed its performance was higher. Our next model was a FNN that included the training of embedding for the Reason for Visit (RFV) codes, performing better than all previous models.

Prediction

The predictor leverages the trained models to generate an ESI rating and associated supporting data.

IDANN follows the official definition of ESI as closely as possible to ensure that its use conforms with established emergency department practices, thus minimizing the implementation risk and allowing for faster deployment with minimal disruption.

Apis

REST API

A client application passes a JSON package with all relevant information to the API, which then executes an instance of the predictor function. The result of the predictor function is then passed back to the API service and ultimately the client application, which will leverage the ESI as a suggestion and provide visualizations explaining the decision.
